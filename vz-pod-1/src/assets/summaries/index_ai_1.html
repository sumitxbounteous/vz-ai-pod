Change #4: Service Credit Requirements Tightened

UNFAVORABLE
Section: What are my rights for dropped calls or interrupted Service?
ORIGINAL TEXT

"If you lose Service in your Coverage Area for more than 4 hours in a row and we're at fault, call us within 80 days and we'll give you a credit for the time lost."

PROPOSED TEXT

"If you lose Service in your Coverage Area for more than 24 hours in a row and we're at fault, call us within 180 days and we'll give you a credit for the time lost."

IMPACT ANALYSIS

⚠️ SIGNIFICANT UNFAVORABLE CHANGE

  • Outage Threshold Increased 6x: From 4 hours to 24 hours before credit eligibility
  • Reporting Window Extended: From 80 days to 180 days (appears favorable but see below)
  • Reduced Customer Protection: Customers can now experience up to 24 hours of service interruption without compensation
  • Net Effect: Makes it significantly harder to qualify for service credits despite longer reporting window

Financial Impact: Customer could lose a full day of paid service without compensation (previously only 4 hours).

RECOMMENDATION

⛔ REJECT or NEGOTIATE: This significantly reduces customer protections. Consider:

  • Request retention of 4-hour threshold
  • If increase necessary, negotiate to 12 hours maximum
  • Request proactive notification for outages exceeding 6 hours
  • Consider automatic credits rather than requiring customer to call

Change #5: New Disclaimer of Warranties Section

UNFAVORABLE
Section: DISCLAIMER OF WARRANTIES (New Section)
NEW SECTION ADDED

"We make no representations or warranties, express or implied, including, to the extent permitted by applicable law, any implied warranty of merchantability or fitness for a particular purpose, about your Service, your wireless device, or any applications you access through your wireless device. We do not warrant that your wireless device will work perfectly, that all features will work, that your device will not need occasional upgrades or modifications, or that it will not be negatively affected by network–related modifications, upgrades or similar activity."

IMPACT ANALYSIS

⚠️ MAJOR UNFAVORABLE ADDITION

  • Eliminates Implied Warranties: Specifically disclaims merchantability and fitness for particular purpose
  • No Performance Guarantees: Explicitly states device may not "work perfectly" or that "all features will work"
  • Shifts Risk to Customer: Customer bears all risk of service/device performance issues
  • Broad Scope: Covers service, device, AND third-party applications
  • Network Changes: No warranty against negative effects from Verizon's own network modifications

Legal Impact: Significantly limits customer's ability to seek remedies for defective service or devices.

RECOMMENDATION

⛔ STRONGLY OBJECT: This is an entirely new section that substantially weakens customer protections. Recommendations:

  • Request Removal: This section wasn't in the original agreement
  • If Unavoidable: Add carve-outs for gross negligence or willful misconduct
  • Limit Scope: Exclude disclaimers related to Verizon's own network modifications
  • Add Minimum Standards: Require service to meet "commercially reasonable standards"
  • Legal Review: Consult legal counsel on enforceability in your jurisdiction

⚠️ Note: State laws may limit enforceability of such broad warranty disclaimers, particularly for consumer contracts.

Change #6: Arbitration Settlement Threshold Modified

UNFAVORABLE
Section: Dispute Resolution - Paragraph (5)
ORIGINAL TEXT

"...OR IF WE DON'T MAKE YOU AN OFFER, AND THE ARBITRATOR AWARDS YOU ANY AMOUNT OF MONEY BUT LESS THAN $1,000, THEN WE AGREE TO PAY YOU $5,000 INSTEAD..."

PROPOSED TEXT

"...OR IF WE DON'T MAKE YOU AN OFFER, AND THE ARBITRATOR AWARDS YOU ANY AMOUNT OF MONEY BUT LESS THAN $5,000, THEN WE AGREE TO PAY YOU $5,000 INSTEAD..."

IMPACT ANALYSIS

⚠️ UNFAVORABLE CHANGE - Reduces Customer Protection

Original Version Protection:

  • Award range $1 - $999 → Customer gets $5,000
  • Incentivized Verizon to make fair settlement offers
  • Protected small claims with significant penalty

Proposed Version Impact:

  • Eliminates $5,000 guarantee for awards $1,000 - $4,999
  • Customer now gets only actual award (not $5,000 bonus) for this range
  • Reduces Verizon's incentive to settle small claims fairly
  • Makes arbitration less attractive for mid-range claims

Financial Impact: Customers with valid claims between $1,000-$4,999 lose the $5,000 guarantee, potentially losing thousands in recovery.

RECOMMENDATION

⛔ REJECT: This change significantly weakens customer protections in arbitration. Options:

  • Retain Original Language: Keep $1,000 threshold for $5,000 guarantee
  • Compromise Position: Split the difference at $2,500 threshold
  • Alternative Protection: Add automatic attorneys' fees for awards under $5,000
  • Escalating Scale: Graduated payment (e.g., 2x award for claims under $2,500)

Strategic Note: This provision incentivizes early settlement. Weakening it may increase arbitration costs for both parties.

Change #7: Mass Arbitration Case Selection

NEUTRAL
Section: Dispute Resolution - Paragraph (6)
ORIGINAL TEXT

"IN THE SECOND STAGE, 80 ARBITRATIONS WILL PROCEED, AND COUNSEL FOR THE VERIZON CUSTOMERS AND COUNSEL FOR VERIZON SHALL EACH SELECT 10 CASES TO PROCEED..."

PROPOSED TEXT

"IN THE SECOND STAGE, 80 ARBITRATIONS WILL PROCEED, AND COUNSEL FOR THE VERIZON CUSTOMERS AND COUNSEL FOR VERIZON SHALL EACH SELECT 40 CASES TO PROCEED..."

IMPACT ANALYSIS

This change affects the mass arbitration "bellwether" process for handling 50+ similar claims:

Mathematical Correction:

  • Original stated "80 arbitrations" but only allocated 20 cases (10 + 10)
  • New version corrects this to 80 cases total (40 + 40)
  • Aligns selection with stated total

Practical Impact:

  • Both sides get equal selection rights (40 each)
  • More cases tested in second bellwether round
  • Better data for settlement negotiations
  • Neutral impact - applies equally to both parties
RECOMMENDATION

Accept: This appears to be a correction to align case selection with the stated total. The change maintains balance between parties and may actually benefit customers by:

  • Allowing more customer cases to be heard in bellwether proceedings
  • Providing better statistical data for settlement leverage
  • Maintaining equal selection rights

Change #8: Assignment Rights Clarification

NEUTRAL
Section: About this Agreement
NEW TEXT ADDED

"You cannot assign this Agreement or any of your rights or duties under it without our permission. However, we may assign this Agreement or any debt you owe us without notifying you."

IMPACT ANALYSIS

This addition explicitly addresses assignment rights:

Customer Restrictions:

  • Customer cannot transfer/assign agreement without Verizon approval
  • Standard provision in consumer service contracts
  • Protects Verizon from unauthorized service transfers

Verizon Rights:

  • Verizon can assign agreement without notice
  • Allows for corporate restructuring, mergers, debt sales
  • Typical in commercial contracts
  • Customer continues with same service terms regardless

This is standard boilerplate language common in telecommunications agreements. While asymmetric, it reflects typical business practice and doesn't materially change customer rights.

RECOMMENDATION

Accept with Awareness: This is standard industry language. Consider requesting:

  • Notice requirement when Verizon assigns to third parties (not affiliates)
  • Right to cancel within 30 days of assignment to non-affiliate
  • Assurance that terms won't change upon assignment

Note: Without this clause, assignment rights would default to state law, which often allows reasonable restrictions anyway.
